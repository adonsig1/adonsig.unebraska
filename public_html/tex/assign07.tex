\question{
\begin{enumerate}
\item For a differentiable $g : \bR \to \bR$, prove that if
$g$ has only one critical point, a local minimum at $a \in \bR$, then
$g$ has a global minimum at $a$.
\item The previous part is not true in several variables.
Consider $f(x,y) = e^{3x} + y^3 -3ye^x + 1$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item Prove that $(0,1)$ is a  local minimum.\\
 \hint express $f(x,y)$ in terms of $a=e^x-1$ and $b=y-1$.
\item Show that $(0,1)$ is the only critical point.
\item Is $(0,1)$ a global minimum?
\end{enumerate}
\item Can you explain (briefly) what goes wrong in trying to extend your
proof of part (a) to the function in part (b)?
\end{enumerate}
